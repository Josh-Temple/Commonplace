# MBSR primary source extraction

## Scope

This note supports MBSR-related reader-facing pages in Commonplace. It focuses on primary or institutionally reliable sources for MBSR structure, history, curriculum elements, home practice, teacher training, and evidence boundaries.

## Priority questions

- What can be verified about MBSR's origin and UMass / Center for Mindfulness context?
- What can be verified about the typical 8-week structure?
- What can be verified about orientation, weekly sessions, all-day session, home practice, group inquiry, and teacher-led practice?
- What can be verified about body scan, sitting meditation, mindful movement, and informal mindfulness as core elements?
- What can be verified about teacher training or instructor qualification standards?
- What should not be stated as official curriculum without direct support?

## Sources checked

### UMass Memorial Health Center for Mindfulness

- Source name: UMass Memorial Health Center for Mindfulness pages, including the MBSR program page.
- Type: institutional / near-primary historical source.
- What it supports: Jon Kabat-Zinn, PhD, developed MBSR at UMass Memorial Medical Center in 1979; UMass Memorial Health presents the current program as the original 8-week MBSR program and places it in the continuing Center for Mindfulness context.
- What it does not support: exact week-by-week curriculum sequence, manual-level teaching scripts, exact home-practice minutes, or universal claims that MBSR treats a condition for every reader.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: yes, for cautious origin and 8-week-structure language; no, for official curriculum reconstruction.

### Brown University public MBSR program page

- Source name: Brown University School of Professional Studies, Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction.
- Type: institutional.
- What it supports: Brown's public MBSR offering is described as an 8-week program with orientation, one all-day session, instructor-led sessions, reflection/practice, online or in-person format, and 2.5 hours per week in the current public course description.
- What it does not support: exact official MBSR sequence for all institutions, clinical indications, teacher scripts, or exact home-practice requirements for Commonplace readers.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: yes, to say public institutional descriptions commonly include 8 weeks, orientation, weekly instructor-led sessions, home practice, reflection/inquiry, and an all-day or extended session.

### Brown Mindfulness Center / teacher-training pages

- Source name: Brown Mindfulness Center and Brown MBSR Teacher Training pages.
- Type: institutional.
- What it supports: Brown offers a multi-tiered training pathway for MBSR teaching and describes MBSR teaching as requiring practice and teaching skills. Brown's FAQ emphasizes that MBSR teachers need comprehensive training because varied participant experiences can appear in the classroom.
- What it does not support: a universal global teacher-training standard, exact requirements for all MBSR teachers, or the claim that a reader can self-certify from Commonplace pages.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: yes, as a boundary: official programs may involve trained teachers and inquiry; teacher qualification details should remain source-note-only unless a page specifically reviews standards.

### NCCIH meditation and mindfulness effectiveness/safety page

- Source name: NCCIH, “Meditation and Mindfulness: Effectiveness and Safety.”
- Type: governmental / institutional secondary evidence summary.
- What it supports: meditation and mindfulness evidence varies by condition and study quality; meditation should not be used to postpone or replace conventional care; safety evidence is not complete even when practices are often considered low risk.
- What it does not support: MBSR as a cure, universal safety, or condition-specific claims without separate review.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: yes, for cautious safety and evidence-boundary wording.

### Goyal et al. 2014

- Source name: Goyal et al., “Meditation Programs for Psychological Stress and Well-being.”
- Type: peer-reviewed systematic review/meta-analysis.
- What it supports: cautious general discussion of meditation-program evidence in adult clinical populations.
- What it does not support: exact MBSR curriculum structure, individual guarantees, or disease-specific claims in these pages.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: yes, only as cautious evidence-background language.

### Parsons et al. 2017

- Source name: Parsons et al., “Home practice in Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy and Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction.”
- Type: peer-reviewed systematic review/meta-analysis.
- What it supports: home practice is a relevant feature in MBSR/MBCT research, and participants often practice less than assigned amounts.
- What it does not support: a simple “more minutes always equals better outcomes” rule, exact official home-practice instructions, or a safe dose for all readers.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: yes, to discuss home practice cautiously and emphasize safe adjustment rather than dose escalation.

## Candidate source categories

- UMass / Center for Mindfulness / historical institutional materials: partially checked for origin and current institutional framing; not enough for manual-level curriculum.
- Brown University MBSR page: checked for public program structure.
- Brown Mindfulness Center: checked for training/institutional boundary language.
- NCCIH meditation and mindfulness effectiveness/safety page: checked for safety/evidence boundaries.
- Kabat-Zinn primary or near-primary texts: not page-extracted in this pass.
- MBSR teacher training / standards materials: Brown pages checked at public-summary level only.
- Goyal et al. 2014: checked at citation/summary level; no quantitative extraction promoted.
- Parsons et al. 2017: checked at abstract/full-text summary level for home-practice caution.

## Verified claims

- MBSR can be cautiously described as an 8-week structured mindfulness education program.
- UMass Memorial Health attributes MBSR's development to Jon Kabat-Zinn at UMass Memorial Medical Center in 1979.
- Public Brown descriptions support orientation, weekly instructor-led sessions, reflection/practice, home practice, and one all-day session as current institutional program elements.
- Body scan, sitting meditation, mindful movement, and informal mindfulness are safe to describe as common MBSR practice elements when not presented as a complete official curriculum.
- Teacher-led practice and group inquiry/reflection are part of institutional program descriptions; self-guided Commonplace pages should not claim to replace them.
- Home practice is relevant to MBSR/MBCT research, but the relationship between amount and outcome should be stated cautiously.

## Claims to avoid

- Exact official week-by-week curriculum sequence, unless directly verified.
- Exact home-practice requirements, unless directly verified.
- Teacher-training requirements, unless directly verified.
- MBSR cures stress, anxiety, pain, depression, or illness.
- MBSR replaces medical care or psychotherapy.
- MBSR is identical to Buddhist retreat practice.
- More practice minutes always produce better outcomes.

## Reader-facing safe wording

- 「MBSRは、8週間型の構造化されたマインドフルネス教育プログラムとして理解しやすいです。」
- 「このロードマップは公式コースの代替ではなく、構造を理解するための読者向け地図です。」
- 「MBSRの近い目標は、ストレスを消すことではなく、ストレス反応に気づくことです。」
- 「ホームプラクティスは、長く座るためではなく、日常で気づく回数を増やすために使います。」
- 「公式プログラムには、訓練を受けた指導者、グループでの inquiry、参加前の確認、実践中の調整が含まれることがあります。」
- 「公式カリキュラムの詳細や週ごとの正式順序には、まだ未確認の部分があります。」
