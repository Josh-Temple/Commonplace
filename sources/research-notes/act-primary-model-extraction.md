# ACT primary model extraction

## Scope

This note supports ACT-related reader-facing pages in Commonplace. It focuses on ACT's conceptual model, not condition-specific treatment efficacy.

## Priority questions

- How do primary or authoritative ACT sources define psychological flexibility?
- How do they define the six processes?
- How are acceptance, defusion, present-moment contact, self as context, values, and committed action related?
- How do ACT sources distinguish values from goals?
- How do ACT sources describe committed action?
- What boundaries should exist between therapist-guided ACT and ACT-informed self-reflection?

## Sources checked

### ACBS ACT overview page

- Source name: Association for Contextual Behavioral Science ACT overview.
- Type: ACBS / authoritative professional association source.
- What it supports: ACT is defined as a psychological intervention using mindfulness and acceptance processes plus commitment and behavior-change processes to create psychological flexibility.
- What it does not support: self-guided ACT therapy, diagnosis-specific advice, condition-specific efficacy claims, or claims that ACT is simply a meditation program.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: yes, for broad model language and self-practice boundaries.

### ACBS Six Core Processes page

- Source name: Association for Contextual Behavioral Science, “The Six Core Processes of ACT.”
- Type: ACBS / authoritative professional association source.
- What it supports: six processes are acceptance, defusion, contact with the present moment, self as context, values, and committed action; the processes support one another and target psychological flexibility; contact with the present moment and self as context are shared across mindfulness/acceptance and commitment/behavior-change groupings.
- What it does not support: a fixed order of practice, a self-help treatment manual, or condition-specific outcomes.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: yes, for the six-process model and the point that processes are interrelated.

### Hayes et al. 2006

- Source name: Hayes, Luoma, Bond, Masuda, and Lillis, “Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: Model, processes and outcomes.”
- Type: peer-reviewed model/outcome review.
- What it supports: ACT has a model of psychopathology and treatment processes and is linked to relational frame theory and behavioral-process research; the paper is a priority model source.
- What it does not support in this pass: page-numbered or direct-quote claims, because only abstract/summary-level extraction was performed here.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: yes, only as a cited priority model source in source notes; no direct claims beyond ACBS-supported wording should be promoted until page-level extraction is done.

### Hayes, Strosahl, Wilson ACT book

- Source name: Hayes, Strosahl, Wilson, *Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: The Process and Practice of Mindful Change*.
- Type: book / primary professional model text.
- What it supports: priority status for deeper ACT model extraction.
- What it does not support in this pass: page-level definitions, direct quotes, or detailed practice instructions, because the book was not directly page-extracted.
- Whether it can be reflected in `content/`: not beyond naming it as a needed follow-up source.

## Verified claims

- ACT is best described here as a psychological-flexibility model, not as a meditation program.
- ACT uses mindfulness/acceptance processes and commitment/behavior-change processes.
- The six core processes are acceptance, cognitive defusion, contact with the present moment, self as context, values, and committed action.
- The six processes are interrelated and support psychological flexibility; they do not need to be taught to readers as a fixed order.
- Values can be safely presented as chosen directions or qualities of action, distinct from one-time goals and mood-control wishes, when phrased cautiously.
- Committed action can be safely presented as small, observable, values-consistent behavior, not as a grand promise or productivity hack.
- ACT-informed self-reflection should be separated from therapist-guided ACT.

## Claims to avoid

- ACT is a meditation program.
- ACT guarantees symptom reduction.
- ACT lets the reader control or eliminate emotions.
- Acceptance means passive endurance.
- Values are the same as goals.
- Committed action is a productivity hack.
- Self-guided ACT-informed reflection replaces therapy.
- Condition-specific efficacy claims without a separate review.

## Reader-facing safe wording

- 「ACTは瞑想プログラムではなく、心理的柔軟性を高め、価値に沿った行動へつなげるモデルとして理解しやすいです。」
- 「ACT-informedな自己実践は、日常の行動選択を助けるための翻訳です。」
- 「心理療法としてのACTを自己流で置き換えるものではありません。」
- 「受容は我慢や危険の正当化ではありません。」
- 「価値は達成して終わる目標ではなく、行動の方向として扱います。」
- 「コミットメント行動は、大きな決意ではなく、次の小さい観察可能な行動です。」
