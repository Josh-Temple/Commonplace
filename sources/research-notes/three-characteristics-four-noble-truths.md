# Three characteristics and four noble truths

## Scope

This note supports cautious background content about anicca, dukkha, anattā, and the four noble truths in Theravada-related meditation contexts.

It is not a doctrinal authority, clinical protocol, or instruction to intensify no-self, suffering, or impermanence contemplation.

## Three characteristics

- **anicca**: impermanence, instability, change.
- **dukkha**: suffering, unsatisfactoriness, unreliability, or inability of conditioned things to provide final satisfaction depending on context and translation.
- **anattā**: not-self / non-self; not a casual claim that “I do not exist” and not a dissociation practice.

## Four noble truths

- suffering / unsatisfactoriness
- origin
- cessation
- path

## Relationship to meditation

Existing Commonplace notes support saying that Theravada-related meditation is framed by ethics, concentration, wisdom, non-clinging, and liberation-oriented doctrine. Ānāpānasati's final tetrad includes impermanence, fading / dispassion, cessation, and relinquishment; this belongs to Buddhist insight context, not ordinary stress management.

Satipaṭṭhāna dhammas contemplation includes four noble truths in checked primary-text notes. Reader-facing pages can mention this as background, but should not guide readers into strong insight contemplation.

## Reader-facing safe framing

- These are Buddhist doctrinal frames, not clinical tools.
- Do not turn anattā / no-self into dissociation or self-negation.
- Do not turn dukkha contemplation into self-punishment or pessimism.
- Do not use impermanence as emotional bypassing.
- Do not prescribe strong insight contemplation as beginner self-help.
- Use these terms as a map for understanding why meditation is embedded in ethics, concentration, wisdom, and non-clinging.
