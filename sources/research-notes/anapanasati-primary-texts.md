# Ānāpānasati primary texts

## Scope

This note supports cautious reader-facing content about mindfulness of breathing in a Theravada / Pāli-canon context.

It does not provide a full meditation manual, attainment map, breathwork protocol, or clinical-efficacy review.

## Primary text

- MN 118 Ānāpānasati Sutta / Ānāpānassatisutta.

## Sixteen steps

### Body tetrad

- long breath;
- short breath;
- whole body;
- calming bodily fabrication.

Reader-facing caution: this is not simply “take deep breaths.” The practice is framed as knowing or training with the breath, not forcing a breathing pattern.

### Feeling tetrad

- rapture;
- pleasure;
- mental fabrication;
- calming mental fabrication.

Reader-facing caution: these are refined meditative categories and should not be promised as beginner outcomes.

### Mind tetrad

- sensitive to mind;
- satisfying mind;
- steadying mind;
- releasing mind.

Reader-facing caution: do not invite readers to diagnose meditative attainment or force unusual states.

### Dhamma tetrad

- impermanence;
- fading / dispassion;
- cessation;
- relinquishment.

Reader-facing caution: this tetrad has doctrinal and insight context. It should not be reduced to ordinary stress management.

## Relation to four satipaṭṭhānas

MN 118 presents mindfulness of breathing as connected with the four satipaṭṭhānas: body, feeling, mind, and dhammas. It also connects the development of these foundations with the seven awakening factors in the sutta's broader structure.

Reader-facing pages may say that Ānāpānasati is a breathing-based practice that can be read as moving through body, feeling, mind, and dhamma frames. They should not claim that a short breath-awareness practice reproduces the full sixteen-step path.

## Reader-facing safe framing

- This is not simply “deep breathing.”
- It should not be presented as constant breath control.
- Some stages involve refined concentration and doctrinal insight, not beginner self-help.
- For Commonplace, beginner-facing content should focus on noticing breath safely, not reproducing the full sixteen-step path.
- Breath-focused practice should include alternatives for people whose anxiety, panic, trauma response, or body vigilance worsens with breath attention.
