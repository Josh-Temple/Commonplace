# Dual-process theory

## Scope

This source note supports cautious reader-facing content about dual-process theory in the decision-making cluster. It treats System 1 / System 2 language as a practical shorthand, not a complete model of the mind.

## Executive summary

- Dual-process theories commonly distinguish fast/automatic/intuitive processing from slower/controlled/deliberative processing.
- The System 1 / System 2 shorthand is useful for readers, but it can over-simplify diverse theories.
- Critics warn against assuming all mental processes divide neatly into two aligned types.
- Commonplace should use dual-process language as a prompt to pause under emotion, time pressure, or uncertainty, not as a claim that intuition is bad or deliberation is always correct.

## Key claims

### Claim 1: Dual-process accounts are a broad family, not one single theory.

Evidence:

- Evans' 2008 Annual Review article describes a diverse set of dual-processing proposals across reasoning, judgment, and social cognition.
- Evans and Stanovich's 2013 article argues that criticisms sometimes attack “dual-process theories” as if there were one generic version, while actual theories differ.

Uncertainty / limits:

- This note does not map all versions of dual-process theory.
- Reader-facing pages should avoid detailed taxonomies unless a future source review supports them.

### Claim 2: The fast/automatic versus slow/controlled distinction is practically useful but limited.

Evidence:

- Evans' review supports the broad distinction between fast/automatic processes and slower, deliberative processes across several literatures.
- Popular System 1 / System 2 language is useful for explaining why a person might pause before acting under anger, FOMO, or loss pressure.

Uncertainty / limits:

- “System 1” and “System 2” should not be treated as literal locations or fully separate agents.
- Fast processing can be skilled and useful; slow processing can rationalize a preferred conclusion.

### Claim 3: There are important criticisms of simple two-box models.

Evidence:

- Melnikoff and Bargh's “The Mythical Number Two” criticizes the assumption that psychological phenomena can be partitioned into two types.
- Evans and Stanovich acknowledge that some criticisms have force against some dual-process theories, even while arguing that broad dismissals are overstated.

Uncertainty / limits:

- This note does not adjudicate the debate.
- Commonplace should not claim that dual-process theory is settled or that System 2 can fully control System 1.

## Practical implications

- Use dual-process theory as a practical pause cue: “This may be a fast/emotional judgment; run a pre-decision check.”
- Do not tell readers to suppress intuition. Instead, ask when intuition is sufficient and when a record, delay, outside view, or opposing evidence is needed.
- Link to metacognition, cognitive bias, and pre-decision checks rather than treating dual-process language as a standalone explanation.

## Safe wording

- 「二重過程理論は、速い判断と遅い判断を区別する便利な補助線だが、人間の認知を完全に二分するものではない。」
- 「速い判断が常に悪いわけではなく、遅い判断が常に正しいわけでもない。」
- 「Commonplaceでは、衝動的判断に気づいて判断前チェックへ移るための実践上の比喩として使う。」

## Cautions

- Avoid “System 1を完全に制御する.”
- Avoid “直観は悪い / 熟慮は正しい.”
- Avoid presenting Kahneman's popular framing as the whole academic debate.
- Avoid claims that a reader can eliminate automatic processing.
