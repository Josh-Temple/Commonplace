# Meditation and mindfulness overview

This note separates broad definitions from intervention-specific evidence. It should not be read as proof that “meditation” has one uniform effect.

## Working definitions

- **Meditation** is a family of attentional, contemplative, affective, and embodied practices. Common forms include focused attention, open monitoring, body scan, walking meditation, compassion/loving-kindness practices, mantra practices, and movement-based forms.
- **Mindfulness** in modern clinical contexts usually means intentionally attending to present-moment experience with an attitude of curiosity, non-reactivity, and reduced automatic judgment.
- In Buddhist contexts, terms translated as mindfulness, such as *sati*, sit inside broader ethical, philosophical, and liberation-oriented frameworks. They are not identical to secular stress-reduction protocols.

## Main classifications

- Focused attention: stabilizing attention on a chosen object such as breath sensation.
- Open monitoring: noticing arising and passing sensations, thoughts, and emotions without selecting a single narrow object.
- Constructive/affective practices: cultivating compassion, loving-kindness, gratitude, or prosocial intentions.
- Body-based practices: body scan, walking meditation, mindful movement, yoga-like movement in MBSR.

## Evidence tendencies

- NCCIH summarizes meditation and mindfulness research as promising for some outcomes but variable by condition, study quality, comparison group, and intervention type. It emphasizes that meditation is not a substitute for conventional care.
- Goyal et al. (2014) reviewed meditation programs for psychological stress and well-being in adult clinical populations and reported moderate evidence for small improvements in anxiety, depression, and pain, with lower or insufficient evidence for several other outcomes.
- Mechanism reviews, including Gu et al. (2015), suggest possible roles for mindfulness skills, rumination, cognitive/emotional reactivity, worry, and self-compassion, but mediation evidence has methodological limits.

## Limits

- “Meditation works” is too broad. Effects depend on method, dose, teacher skill, motivation, clinical status, and comparison condition.
- Many trials use waitlist or usual-care controls; findings are often weaker against active controls.
- Adverse effects and dropout are not always measured carefully.
