# Decision review and decision journals

## Scope

This source note supports cautious reader-facing content about judgment review, decision journals, forecasting-style records, postmortem/premortem practices, outcome bias, hindsight bias, and separating decision-process quality from outcome quality.

## Executive summary

- Outcome bias research supports the caution that people may evaluate decision quality partly from outcomes known after the fact.
- Hindsight bias research supports separating what was known before the outcome from what is known afterward.
- Decision journals are practical tools for preserving reasons, uncertainty, expectations, and review conditions before outcomes are known, but they are not a guaranteed performance-improvement method.
- Premortem practice asks decision makers to imagine a future failure and generate plausible causes before acting.
- Commonplace should use records to make later review easier, not to create heavy self-criticism or false precision.

## Key claims

### Claim 1: Outcome information can distort evaluation of decision quality.

Evidence:

- Baron and Hershey's 1988 article “Outcome Bias in Decision Evaluation” reports studies in which outcomes affected evaluations of decisions made under uncertainty.
- The PubMed record summarizes that subjects received descriptions and outcomes of decisions under uncertainty.

Uncertainty / limits:

- The studies do not prove that all real-world reviews are outcome-biased in the same way.
- Outcome information is sometimes relevant; the caution is against using outcome alone as decision-quality evidence.

### Claim 2: Hindsight bias makes after-the-fact review difficult.

Evidence:

- Fischhoff's 1975 work introduced early experimental study of hindsight bias using historical vignettes.
- Fischhoff and Beyth's 1975 work on remembered probabilities of once-future events is a key source for the idea that outcome knowledge can affect remembered prior expectations.
- Later reviews describe hindsight bias as involving feeling that an event was more predictable after it is known.

Uncertainty / limits:

- This note does not extract effect sizes or domain-specific findings.
- Hindsight bias is one reason for contemporaneous records, but records themselves can still be incomplete or self-serving.

### Claim 3: Decision journals can preserve pre-outcome reasoning, but evidence for outcome improvement should remain cautious.

Evidence:

- Practitioner decision-journal approaches commonly record the decision, reasons, assumptions, confidence/forecast, uncertainty, and review date before outcome is known.
- Outcome-bias and hindsight-bias research support the rationale for preserving pre-outcome information.

Uncertainty / limits:

- This pass did not locate a strong peer-reviewed RCT showing that ordinary decision journals improve personal outcomes across domains.
- Practitioner sources such as Annie Duke and Ray Dalio are useful candidates, but need direct extraction before stronger claims.
- Trading-performance improvement must not be claimed.

### Claim 4: Premortem / prospective hindsight is a related pre-decision practice.

Evidence:

- Gary Klein's 2007 Harvard Business Review article describes a premortem as imagining that a project has failed and then generating plausible reasons before the project proceeds.
- Premortem practice is related to forecasting and risk identification, not to guaranteeing success.

Uncertainty / limits:

- This note does not verify empirical effect sizes for premortems.
- Premortem should remain a candidate future page, not a validated guarantee.

## Practical implications

- Reader-facing pages may ask readers to record: decision, evidence, opposing evidence, uncertainty, confidence, invalidation/review condition, and expected review date.
- Reviews should separate decision quality from outcome quality.
- Use short records; heavy logs can fail or become self-justification.
- Applications to trading, writing, and career decisions should remain abstract and public-safe.

## Safe wording

- 「判断レビューは、結果だけでなく、判断時点で分かっていた情報、根拠、不確実性、感情、撤退条件を見直すために使う。」
- 「結果が良いことは、判断プロセスが良かったことを必ず意味しない。結果が悪いことも、判断プロセスが悪かったことを必ず意味しない。」
- 「事前に根拠と不確実性を書くと、後から結果論だけで評価するリスクを下げやすい。」
- 「判断日誌は成果改善を保証するものではなく、検証しやすくする道具です。」

## Cautions

- Do not give investment advice or imply trade performance improvement.
- Do not make review forms so heavy that they stop being used.
- Do not let journals become self-justification or self-attack.
- Do not overcorrect from a single outcome.
- Do not treat process/outcome separation as ignoring outcomes entirely.

## Needs verification

- Direct extraction of Fischhoff 1975 and Fischhoff & Beyth 1975.
- Direct extraction of Baron and Hershey 1988.
- Stronger sources on decision journals in forecasting, investing, management, or professional judgment.
- Premortem empirical evidence beyond Klein's practitioner HBR article.
- Dedicated source note for forecasting and calibration if a future page expands that area.
