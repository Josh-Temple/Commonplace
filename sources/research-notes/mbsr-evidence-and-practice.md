# MBSR evidence and practice

## Overview

Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) was developed by Jon Kabat-Zinn in a clinical setting as a structured group program. Contemporary institutional descriptions commonly present it as an 8-week course with instructor-led sessions, home practice, and an extended practice session.

## Typical structure

- Orientation or intake.
- About 8 weekly group sessions.
- Formal practices: body scan, sitting meditation, mindful movement/yoga-like movement.
- Informal practices: bringing attention to ordinary daily activities and stress reactions.
- A longer retreat or all-day practice session in many versions.

## Evidence

- Brown University describes its MBSR offering as an 8-week program for learning mindfulness meditation and movement practices for daily life, with weekly sessions and an all-day session.
- NCCIH summarizes mixed but generally promising evidence for mindfulness-based approaches in some stress-related outcomes, anxiety, depression, and pain, while warning that quality and condition-specific findings vary.
- Goyal et al. (2014) found moderate evidence for small improvements in anxiety, depression, and pain across meditation programs, many of which were mindfulness programs; this should not be converted into a guarantee for MBSR in every population.
- Parsons et al. (2017) reviewed home practice in MBSR/MBCT and its relationship with outcomes; home practice may matter, but adherence and measurement vary.

## Cautions

- MBSR is not a replacement for medical treatment.
- Claims should distinguish MBSR from generic relaxation, breathing exercises, MBCT, ACT, or Buddhist retreat practice.
- More intensive practice is not automatically better for every person.
