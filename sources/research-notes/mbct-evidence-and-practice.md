# MBCT evidence and practice

## Overview

Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) integrates mindfulness practices with elements of cognitive therapy. It is most often discussed in relation to prevention of depressive relapse or recurrence among people with recurrent depression who are currently well enough to participate.

## Mechanisms and targets

- Recognizing thoughts as mental events rather than facts.
- Reducing automatic rumination and cognitive reactivity.
- Developing decentering/metacognitive awareness.
- Building relapse-prevention skills rather than promising permanent mood control.

## Evidence

- Kuyken et al. (2016) individual patient data meta-analysis included 9 trials and 1,329 participants and found MBCT reduced depressive relapse risk versus usual care over 60 weeks, with outcomes comparable to other active treatments in that analysis.
- Kuyken et al. (2015) PREVENT trial compared MBCT with support to taper/discontinue antidepressants against maintenance antidepressants for relapse/recurrence prevention; the trial did not show superiority of MBCT over maintenance antidepressants, but suggested MBCT may be a viable option for some people under appropriate care.
- Piet & Hougaard (2011) meta-analysis supports MBCT as relapse prevention for recurrent major depressive disorder, especially in people with multiple previous episodes; do not generalize to all depression presentations.

## Limits

- MBCT is not simply MBSR with a different name: it uses cognitive therapy framing and relapse prevention.
- It should not be presented as a self-treatment for acute severe depression, suicidality, mania, psychosis, or complex trauma.
- Current symptom severity, medication status, and clinical supervision matter.

## 2026-06-24 deeper verification pass

### NICE recommendation text versus rationale

- NICE NG222 recommendations section 1.8 addresses preventing relapse after full or partial remission.
- Recommendation 1.8.5 says that for people remitted after antidepressant medication alone and assessed as higher risk of relapse, options to consider include continuing antidepressants, a course of psychological therapy such as group CBT or MBCT for people who do not wish to continue antidepressants, or continuing antidepressants plus group CBT/MBCT.
- Recommendation 1.8.6 says group CBT or MBCT for relapse prevention should explicitly focus on relapse-prevention skills and usually consists of 8 sessions over 2 to 3 months, with optional additional sessions over the next 12 months.
- NICE rationale states that SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, group CBT, and MBCT had evidence for relapse prevention and were on average cost-effective for people at high risk of relapse, with data up to 2 years.

### Recommendation / rationale / service materials

- Recommendation text: use for cautious content about higher-risk relapse prevention after remission and explicit relapse-prevention skills.
- Rationale text: use for why NICE included MBCT among relapse-prevention options.
- Oxford/NHS/service/referrer materials: use as supplementary context, not as the primary guideline wording.

### Reader-facing language allowed

- “MBCT is associated especially with relapse-prevention contexts for recurrent depression or people at higher relapse risk, under appropriate support.”
- “MBCT is not a self-directed replacement for care during acute severe depression, suicidality, mania, psychosis, or complex trauma activation.”
