# Five precepts

## Scope

This note supports cautious reader-facing content about the five precepts as Buddhist ethical training, especially in Theravada-related lay and meditation contexts.

It is not a full vinaya study, ordination guide, legal ethics guide, universal moral theory, or clinical-efficacy review.

## Five precepts

The five precepts are commonly presented as abstaining from:

- killing living beings
- taking what is not given
- sexual misconduct
- false speech
- intoxicants that cause heedlessness

## Relationship to meditation

The five precepts support the existing Commonplace framing that Theravada-related meditation is not merely attention training.

They help connect meditation with ethics, speech, conduct, heedfulness, and the conditions that make the mind more or less stable.

Reader-facing pages may use the five precepts as background for ethical training and harm-reduction boundaries, but should not turn them into moral superiority, self-hatred, or judgment of others.

## Reader-facing safe framing

- Treat the precepts as ethical training and harm-reduction boundaries.
- Do not turn them into self-hatred, moral superiority, or judgment of others.
- Do not collapse Buddhist ethics into modern productivity or self-control hacks.
- Do not present the five precepts as a complete solution to modern legal, medical, interpersonal, or professional ethical problems.
- Discuss lay practice cautiously and avoid pretending to settle sectarian or translation differences.
- Use the precepts to show that meditation is not isolated from speech, conduct, livelihood, and heedfulness.
