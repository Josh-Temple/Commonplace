# Theravada meditation overview

## Scope

This note supports reader-facing pages about Theravada Buddhist meditation in Commonplace. It is not a doctrinal authority, meditation manual, ordination guide, or clinical-efficacy review.

It supports cautious distinctions among Pāli-canon texts, Theravada-related traditions, modern mindfulness programs, psychological interventions, and Commonplace educational self-practice.

## Core distinction

- **Buddhist practice context**: meditation is embedded in ethics, wisdom, non-clinging, liberation, monastic/lay community, and teacher-guided training.
- **Modern mindfulness context**: practices may be translated into attention training, stress reactivity, emotion regulation, or daily awareness, but this translation is partial.
- **Clinical / psychological intervention context**: MBSR, MBCT, ACT, loving-kindness interventions, and compassion interventions require their own manuals, populations, outcomes, comparison groups, and safety evidence.
- **Self-practice / educational context in Commonplace**: pages may offer maps, vocabulary, safe boundaries, and short ordinary-life reflections, not traditional attainment claims or clinical treatment claims.

## Main practice families

- **satipaṭṭhāna**: four foundations / frames of reference: body, feeling tone, mind, and dhammas.
- **ānāpānasati**: mindfulness of breathing, especially as described in MN 118 with sixteen steps and links to the four satipaṭṭhānas.
- **mettā / brahmavihāra**: goodwill / loving-kindness and the four brahmavihāras: mettā, karuṇā, muditā, upekkhā.
- **samatha**: serenity, tranquility, settling, or calming cultivation; should not be reduced to relaxation.
- **vipassanā**: insight or clear seeing; should not be reduced to generic open monitoring.
- **jhāna**: absorption or deep concentration states in Buddhist meditative contexts; not a casual productivity technique.
- **contemplation of body**: breath, postures, activities, body parts, elements, and cemetery contemplations in Satipaṭṭhāna contexts.
- **contemplation of feeling tone**: pleasant, painful, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant experience.
- **contemplation of mind**: recognizing qualities or states of mind such as with/without passion, aversion, delusion, concentration, or liberation.
- **contemplation of dhammas**: includes frameworks such as hindrances, aggregates, sense bases, awakening factors, and noble truths.

## Key doctrinal frames

- Four noble truths.
- Noble eightfold path.
- Five hindrances.
- Seven awakening factors.
- Three characteristics: anicca, dukkha, anattā.
- Non-clinging / release context.

These frames are religious, philosophical, and practical Buddhist contexts. They should not be turned into clinical-outcome evidence.

## Modern translation cautions

- Do not reduce sati to stress reduction.
- Do not reduce vipassanā to generic open monitoring.
- Do not reduce mettā to positive emotion induction.
- Do not collapse samatha and vipassanā into unrelated techniques.
- Do not use suttas as clinical-effect evidence.
- Do not present jhāna as a casual concentration hack or diagnose attainments.
- Do not make beginner self-practice pages reproduce cemetery contemplations, body-parts contemplation, strong no-self contemplation, or intensive retreat practice.
