# Buddhist ethics and precepts

## Scope

This note supports cautious reader-facing content about the five precepts and Buddhist ethical framing in Commonplace.

It is not a full Vinaya study, ordination manual, legal ethics guide, universal moral theory, addiction-treatment resource, or clinical-efficacy review.

## Five precepts

The five precepts are commonly summarized as training principles to:

- refrain from killing;
- refrain from taking what is not given;
- refrain from sexual misconduct;
- refrain from false speech;
- refrain from intoxicants that lead to heedlessness.

Use wording according to directly checked sources and mark translation differences where needed.

## Reader-facing framing

- Precepts as training principles.
- Precepts as harm-reduction and heedfulness supports.
- Precepts as support for judgment and restraint.
- Not as self-punishment or public moral ranking.
- Not as legal advice or universal moral coercion.
- Not as a complete answer to modern interpersonal, professional, medical, legal, or addiction-related problems.

## Relationship to meditation

- Ethics is part of a wider Buddhist path.
- Meditation is not isolated from conduct, speech, livelihood, and intention.
- Precepts can be used to show that attention training is not the whole Buddhist context.
- Do not collapse precepts into productivity rules or private self-control hacks.

## Reader-facing safe wording

- 「五戒は、自分や他人を裁くための道具ではありません。」
- 「Commonplaceでは、害を減らし、軽率な行動を防ぎ、判断を守るための仏教倫理上の訓練原則として慎重に整理します。」
- 「法律、医療、心理療法、対人トラブルの解決策として扱いません。」
- 「五戒は、瞑想を言葉・行動・生活・不注意の条件から切り離さないための背景です。」

## Claims to avoid

- Five precepts as commandments used to rank people morally.
- Five precepts as a universal answer to modern legal, medical, professional, or interpersonal dilemmas.
- Five precepts as addiction treatment or medical advice.
- Fifth precept claims that go beyond checked sources.
- Collapsing lay precepts, uposatha / retreat precepts, and monastic Vinaya.
- Treating ethical conduct as proof of clinical benefit.
