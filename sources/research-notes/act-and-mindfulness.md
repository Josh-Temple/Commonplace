# ACT and mindfulness

## Overview

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) is a contextual behavioral therapy oriented around psychological flexibility. It uses acceptance and mindfulness processes together with commitment and behavior-change processes.

## Six processes

ACBS describes six core ACT processes: acceptance, cognitive defusion, contact with the present moment, self as context, values, and committed action. The point is not to make unpleasant feelings disappear, but to help a person persist or change behavior in the service of chosen values.

## Relationship to mindfulness

- Shared ground: present-moment contact, observing thoughts as thoughts, openness to experience.
- Difference from MBSR: ACT is a psychotherapy model organized around psychological flexibility and values-based action, not primarily an 8-week mindfulness education program.
- Difference from MBCT: ACT does not center only on depressive relapse prevention; it uses a broader process model.

## Evidence caution

This note only supports basic conceptual claims about ACT and mindfulness. Condition-specific efficacy claims require separate source review.
