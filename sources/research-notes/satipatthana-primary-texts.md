# Satipaṭṭhāna primary texts

## Scope

This note supports cautious reader-facing content about Satipaṭṭhāna. It summarizes primary text structure without claiming clinical efficacy.

It is not a full translation, doctrinal authority, or meditation manual.

## Primary texts

- MN 10 Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta / Satipaṭṭhānasutta.
- DN 22 Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta / Mahāsatipaṭṭhānasutta.
- Parallel references were not reviewed in this pass.

## Four foundations / frames of reference

### Body

The body section includes:

- breathing;
- postures;
- clear comprehension in activities;
- body parts;
- elements;
- cemetery contemplations.

Reader-facing caution: breath, posture, and ordinary activity awareness are easier to reference safely. Body-parts and cemetery contemplations should not be promoted as beginner self-guided exercises.

### Feeling tone

The feeling-tone section includes:

- pleasant;
- painful;
- neither-painful-nor-pleasant;
- worldly / unworldly distinctions appear in translations and need careful wording if used.

Reader-facing caution: “feeling” here is closer to affective tone or hedonic tone, not a full emotional story.

### Mind

The mind section includes recognizing qualities such as:

- mind with / without passion;
- mind with / without aversion;
- mind with / without delusion;
- contracted, scattered, concentrated, liberated, and related states depending on translation.

Reader-facing caution: this is noticing the state of mind, not rumination, self-diagnosis, or personality judgment.

### Dhammas

The dhammas section includes:

- five hindrances;
- five aggregates;
- six sense bases;
- seven awakening factors;
- four noble truths.

Reader-facing caution: “dhammas” has multiple translation possibilities and includes doctrinal frameworks. It should not be collapsed into “thoughts” or modern psychological laws.

## Reader-facing safe framing

- This is a map of contemplative domains, not a quick relaxation technique.
- Some practices, especially body-parts, cemetery contemplations, and intense observation of mental states, may be unsuitable for self-guided beginners.
- Use clinical safety boundaries from the mindfulness safety pages.
- Liberation-oriented claims in the suttas should be treated as religious / doctrinal claims, not clinical evidence.

## Translation and interpretation cautions

- “sati” / “mindfulness” has wider Buddhist context than modern attention training.
- “dhammas” has multiple translation possibilities.
- The sutta's liberation claims should be treated as religious / doctrinal claims, not clinical evidence.
- DN 22 overlaps with MN 10 but includes expanded material; this note does not settle their historical or textual relationship.
