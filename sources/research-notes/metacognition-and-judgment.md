# Metacognition and judgment

## Scope

This source note supports cautious reader-facing content about metacognition, confidence monitoring, calibration, and judgment review. It translates research concepts into practical self-monitoring prompts without claiming that metacognition guarantees correct decisions.

## Executive summary

- Metacognition broadly concerns knowledge, monitoring, and control of one's own cognitive processes.
- Metacognitive monitoring includes judgments about confidence, knowing, learning, memory, and performance.
- Calibration concerns the relationship between confidence and accuracy or performance.
- Metacognition may support better learning and judgment when linked to feedback and control, but it can fail under fatigue, emotion, time pressure, poor information, or self-justification.
- Commonplace should connect metacognition to pre-decision checks and decision reviews.

## Key claims

### Claim 1: Metacognition involves monitoring and regulating cognition.

Evidence:

- Flavell's 1979 article is a foundational source on metacognition and cognitive monitoring.
- Nelson and Narens' metamemory framework distinguishes meta-level monitoring and control in relation to object-level cognition.
- Dunlosky and Metcalfe's textbook treats metacognition as evaluating and controlling cognitive processes across applied, developmental, educational, and cognitive psychology.

Uncertainty / limits:

- This note does not verify every subfield definition.
- Reader-facing content should avoid collapsing educational metacognition, clinical self-awareness, and everyday judgment into one identical construct.

### Claim 2: Confidence monitoring and calibration are relevant to judgment.

Evidence:

- Metacognition literature includes judgments of learning, feeling of knowing, confidence, and monitoring accuracy.
- Calibration language is appropriate when discussing whether confidence tracks available evidence or later performance.

Uncertainty / limits:

- This note does not provide a validated calibration protocol for Commonplace readers.
- Confidence can be influenced by fluency, emotion, social pressure, or memory distortion.

### Claim 3: Metacognition can support better control, but it is not always reliable.

Evidence:

- Nelson and Narens' framework links monitoring to control, supporting the idea that noticing uncertainty can influence subsequent study or action choices.
- Educational and cognitive research often studies how monitoring accuracy affects strategy selection and learning.

Uncertainty / limits:

- Fatigue, strong emotion, time pressure, sleep loss, and incentives can impair self-monitoring.
- Over-monitoring can become rumination or action paralysis.
- Metacognitive awareness alone does not guarantee correct decisions.

## Practical implications

- Reader-facing pages may ask: “What am I feeling?”, “How confident am I?”, “What evidence would change my mind?”, and “Am I under time pressure?”
- Judgment review should separate confidence at the time of decision from outcome known later.
- Metacognition should be paired with records, feedback, and lightweight rules rather than endless introspection.

## Safe wording

- 「メタ認知は、自分の認知状態を観察・評価する働きとして扱える。」
- 「確信度を見ることは有用だが、高い確信が正しさを保証するわけではない。」
- 「疲労、強い感情、時間圧ではメタ認知が弱まりうるため、判断前チェックや記録に支えさせる。」
- 「メタ認知は判断改善の候補要因であり、万能の正解装置ではない。」

## Cautions

- Do not claim that “high metacognition means good judgment.”
- Do not present Commonplace prompts as validated psychological measures.
- Do not encourage excessive self-monitoring for every trivial decision.
- Do not mix clinical assessment, educational measurement, and casual journaling without boundaries.
