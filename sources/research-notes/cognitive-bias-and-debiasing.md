# Cognitive bias and debiasing

## Scope

This source note supports cautious reader-facing pages about cognitive bias, heuristics, and debiasing in the Commonplace decision-making cluster. It supports practical links to pre-decision checks and decision reviews, not claims that knowing bias names reliably improves outcomes.

## Executive summary

- Heuristics-and-biases research describes systematic patterns in judgment under uncertainty, including availability, representativeness, anchoring, and related judgment shortcuts.
- Confirmation bias can be described as seeking or interpreting evidence in ways that favor existing beliefs or hypotheses.
- Prospect theory supports cautious language that gains and losses can be evaluated asymmetrically, but this should not be turned into trading advice or a universal numerical claim.
- Debiasing is difficult; naming a bias is not enough. Practical pages should connect bias awareness to opposing evidence, pre-decision checks, records, feedback, and review.
- Biases should be framed as human information-processing tendencies, not as proof that a person is foolish.

## Key claims

### Claim 1: Cognitive biases are studied as systematic tendencies in judgment under uncertainty.

Evidence:

- Tversky and Kahneman's 1974 Science article describes heuristics used in judgments under uncertainty, including representativeness, availability, and anchoring/adjustment.
- The PubMed record for the same article summarizes the three heuristics and their relation to judgments under uncertainty.
- Stanovich and West's work on reasoning and the rationality debate is relevant for treating heuristics-and-biases findings as part of a broader debate about normative standards, task construal, and individual differences.

Uncertainty / limits:

- This note does not extract every experiment or effect size.
- Heuristics are not always errors; they may be useful shortcuts in some environments.
- Different tasks, norms, and contexts can change whether a response should be called biased.

### Claim 2: Confirmation bias involves favoring existing beliefs, expectations, or hypotheses when seeking or interpreting evidence.

Evidence:

- Nickerson's 1998 review, “Confirmation Bias: A Ubiquitous Phenomenon in Many Guises,” defines confirmation bias in terms of seeking or interpreting evidence in ways partial to existing beliefs, expectations, or a hypothesis in hand.
- The review treats confirmation bias as multi-form rather than as one simple mistake.

Uncertainty / limits:

- This note does not verify all mechanisms or domains covered by Nickerson.
- “Confirmation bias” should not be used as a generic insult or as proof that a specific person is wrong.

### Claim 3: Availability, anchoring, and loss-related framing are useful practical warning labels, but not complete explanations of any one judgment.

Evidence:

- Tversky and Kahneman's availability work and 1974 review support describing availability as judging frequency or probability partly from how easily instances or scenarios come to mind.
- Tversky and Kahneman's 1974 article supports cautious anchoring-and-adjustment language.
- Kahneman and Tversky's 1979 prospect theory paper critiques expected utility theory as a descriptive model and develops an alternative in which gains and losses are central to evaluation under risk.

Uncertainty / limits:

- Loss aversion strength varies by context, task, population, and elicitation method; do not use unverified ratios.
- Anchoring and availability are not the only possible explanations for a judgment.
- Trading, investing, medical, or legal applications require domain-specific evidence and professional context.

### Claim 4: Debiasing requires more than learning bias names.

Evidence:

- Larrick's debiasing chapter and later debiasing reviews organize interventions beyond simple awareness, such as changing the decision maker, improving tools, using alternative perspectives, and changing environments.
- Milkman, Chugh, and Bazerman argue that decision-improvement research should focus on strategies for improving bounded judgment, not only cataloging biases.
- Morewedge and colleagues' debiasing-training work suggests that structured training with feedback can reduce some biases in studied settings, but it should not be generalized to all decisions.

Uncertainty / limits:

- Debiasing evidence varies by bias, task, intervention, outcome, and follow-up period.
- Public self-help content should not claim that a checklist guarantees better outcomes.
- Bias reduction in experimental tasks does not automatically transfer to personal decisions, trading, health, or work.

## Practical implications

- Reader-facing pages may say that bias awareness should be connected to a behavior: seek opposing evidence, write assumptions, define invalidation/review conditions, or run a short pre-decision check.
- Avoid treating bias labels as diagnoses or as ways to criticize other people.
- The decision-making cluster should separate research concepts from practical heuristics and personal operating rules.

## Safe wording

- 「認知バイアスは、愚かさではなく、人間の情報処理に起こりうる傾向として扱う。」
- 「バイアス名を知るだけでは判断改善を保証しない。反対根拠、判断前チェック、判断後レビューへ接続する。」
- 「損失回避、アンカリング、利用可能性は、判断を見直すための警告ラベルであり、単独で原因を断定するものではない。」
- 「判断の成果改善は、対象領域、記録、フィードバック、環境設計によって変わる。」

## Cautions

- Do not write that “research proves” a reader will make better decisions by reading about biases.
- Do not use exact effect sizes, loss-aversion ratios, or intervention impacts unless directly extracted.
- Do not frame cognitive bias as a moral flaw.
- Do not imply financial, medical, legal, or career outcomes will improve.
