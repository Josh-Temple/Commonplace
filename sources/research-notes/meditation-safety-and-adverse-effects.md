# Meditation safety and adverse effects

## Main point

Meditation and mindfulness are often low-risk when brief, voluntary, and appropriately matched to the person, but adverse or unwanted experiences are documented. Safety information belongs in reader-facing pages.

## Reported problems

Reported meditation-related adverse effects include anxiety or panic, emotional flooding, sleep disruption, depersonalization/derealization, dissociation, traumatic re-experiencing, unusual perceptual experiences, mania/hypomania-like activation, pain, dizziness, and functional impairment. Causality and prevalence vary by study design.

## Evidence notes

- NCCIH warns that meditation should not be used to postpone seeing a health care provider and notes that adverse effects have been reported.
- Farias et al. (2020) systematically reviewed meditation adverse events and found that adverse events are reported across meditation practices and meditation-based therapies, but prevalence estimates are hard to interpret because methods differ.
- Britton/Lindahl/colleagues have studied meditation-related challenges and adverse effects in mindfulness-based programs; some participants report negative valence, functional impact, or longer-lasting impairment.
- Hirshberg, Goldberg, Rosenkranz, and Davidson reported that MBSR was not associated with higher harm rates than no-treatment controls in their analysis, which prevents a simplistic “MBSR is dangerous” message.

## Practical safety implications

- Start with short sessions.
- Do not force breath depth or breath rate; breath manipulation can worsen hyperventilation, panic, or body-focused vigilance in some people.
- Use grounding through external senses, movement, eyes open, or stopping practice when internal focus becomes destabilizing.
- People with trauma, dissociation, bipolar/mania risk, psychosis symptoms, severe depression, suicidality, or worsening sleep should use professional support rather than self-prescribing intensive practice.

## Source note status

Working research note, not a reader-facing article. Claims promoted into `content/` should remain weaker than the cited sources.

## 2026-06-24 verification update

- NCCIH safety language was rechecked: meditation is usually considered low risk, but harmful effects are not sufficiently studied for definite safety statements, and meditation should not delay conventional care.
- Farias et al. and Britton/Lindahl-related work remain support for cautious adverse-event language.
- Hirshberg/Goldberg/Rosenkranz/Davidson remains useful to avoid the opposite overstatement that MBSR is categorically harmful.

## Still insufficient

- More detail is needed on screening, trauma-sensitive adaptations, dissociation, mania/psychosis cautions, and sleep-worsening management.
- Do not add prevalence numbers to reader-facing pages without closer review of denominators and measurement methods.
