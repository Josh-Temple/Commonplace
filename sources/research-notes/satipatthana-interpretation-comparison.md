# Satipaṭṭhāna interpretation comparison

## Scope

This note supports cautious reader-facing content about Satipaṭṭhāna interpretation. It is not a doctrinal authority or meditation manual.

It compares checked primary-text and secondary-source entry points only lightly. It should not be used to make strong claims about the final meaning of sati, satipaṭṭhāna, or any lineage's practice instructions.

## Sources / interpreters to compare

- MN 10 Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta on SuttaCentral.
- DN 22 Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta on SuttaCentral.
- Access to Insight translations by Thanissaro Bhikkhu and Soma Thera.
- Bhikkhu Anālayo, *Satipaṭṭhāna: The Direct Path to Realization*, checked only at a high level as a detailed scholarly / practice-oriented study.
- Nyanaponika Thera, *The Heart of Buddhist Meditation*, checked only at a high level as a classic exposition of Satipaṭṭhāna and mental training.
- Bhikkhu Bodhi, checked through SuttaCentral MN 118 translation and existing Commonplace notes; broader Bodhi interpretation still needs deeper review.
- Other sources only if directly checked in future passes.

## Comparison points

### How “sati” / mindfulness is translated or framed

SuttaCentral and Access to Insight translations commonly render sati in ways related to mindfulness, being mindful, or establishing mindfulness. Reader-facing pages should say that sati has a Buddhist training context wider than present-moment attention.

Nyanaponika's framing emphasizes the Buddha's way of mindfulness and mental training. Anālayo's book appears to provide detailed textual analysis and practice interpretation, but this note has not extracted chapter-level claims. Bhikkhu Bodhi needs deeper review before attributing interpretive claims beyond checked translations.

### How “satipaṭṭhāna” is translated or framed

The term is often rendered as foundations, establishments, or frames of reference of mindfulness. These renderings are not interchangeable in nuance. Commonplace can avoid choosing a final translation by using “四念処 / Satipaṭṭhāna” and explaining the four domains.

### How the four foundations are explained

The checked primary-text notes support four domains: body, feeling tone, mind, and dhammas. Reader-facing pages should not collapse these into only breath, thoughts, or emotion awareness.

### How body contemplation is treated

MN 10 / DN 22 include breath, posture, activities, body parts, elements, and cemetery contemplations. Commonplace should mention the range but avoid presenting body-parts, cemetery, or charnel-ground contemplations as casual self-help.

### How feeling tone is treated

Feeling tone should be explained as pleasant, unpleasant, and neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant tone, not full emotional narrative. The worldly / unworldly distinction remains a source-note topic unless carefully sourced.

### How mind contemplation is treated

Mind contemplation includes noticing qualities or conditions of mind. Reader-facing wording should avoid personality judgment, diagnosis, or rumination.

### How dhammas are treated

Dhammas contemplation includes doctrinal / analytical frameworks such as five hindrances, aggregates, sense bases, seven awakening factors, and four noble truths. It is not merely “watch your thoughts.”

### How liberation claims are framed

Liberation claims in Buddhist texts should be treated as religious and doctrinal claims. They are not clinical efficacy evidence and should not be converted into mental-health outcomes.

### What is suitable for beginner-facing Commonplace pages

- A light map of body, feeling tone, mind, and dhammas.
- Explicit boundaries around difficult contemplations.
- Links to safety pages when observation intensifies distress.
- Five hindrances as state-noticing vocabulary, not moral self-attack.

### What should remain source-note only

- Detailed debates about the exact translation of sati / satipaṭṭhāna.
- Detailed Anālayo / Nyanaponika / Bodhi disagreements.
- Liberation-path claims, attainment claims, and retreat instructions.
- Cemetery contemplation, body-parts contemplation, or strong no-self contemplation instructions.

## Reader-facing safe wording

- 「四念処は、身体・感受・心・法という観察領域を整理する地図として参照します。」
- 「ここでいう法の観察には、五蓋・七覚支・四聖諦のような教理的枠組みも含まれるため、単なる思考観察に縮小しません。」
- 「五蓋は、悪い自分を責めるためではなく、心を覆っている状態に気づくための語彙として参照します。」
- 「四念処は現代マインドフルネスと重なる部分がありますが、臨床効果の根拠として扱いません。」

## Claims to avoid

- Treating one modern interpreter as the final authority.
- Treating Satipaṭṭhāna as generic mindfulness.
- Treating Satipaṭṭhāna as clinical evidence.
- Turning cemetery contemplation or body-parts contemplation into casual self-help.
- Treating dhammas contemplation as merely “watching thoughts.”
