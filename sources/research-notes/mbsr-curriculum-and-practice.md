# MBSR curriculum and practice

## Scope

This note supports cautious reader-facing content about the structure and practice elements of MBSR. It is a working source note, not a substitute for instructor training materials or an official MBSR curriculum manual.

## What is verified

- Brown University public MBSR structure: current public descriptions support describing MBSR as an 8-week program with orientation, weekly instructor-led sessions, home practice, and an all-day or extended practice session.
- Brown Mindfulness Center public descriptions: Brown publicly presents mindfulness education offerings including MBSR and MBCT.
- NCCIH cautious effectiveness/safety language: meditation and mindfulness should not replace or delay conventional care, and evidence varies by condition and study quality.
- Goyal et al. 2014: supports cautious, general language about meditation programs and small improvements for some outcomes in adult clinical populations, not guaranteed MBSR-specific effects for every reader.
- Parsons et al. 2017: supports treating home practice as relevant in MBSR/MBCT research, while avoiding simple dose-response claims because measurement, practice type, adherence, and causality vary.
- UMass / Center for Mindfulness: broad historical association with Jon Kabat-Zinn and the UMass clinical context is known, but this pass did not verify manual-level sequencing or teacher-training standards from primary UMass materials.

## Typical MBSR elements

- orientation / intake
- weekly group sessions
- body scan
- sitting meditation
- mindful movement
- informal mindfulness
- home practice
- all-day or extended practice session
- group reflection / inquiry

## Eight-week learning themes

Source-supported public descriptions verify an 8-week structure and common practice elements, but not a single official week-by-week curriculum sequence for this repository.

Reader-facing pages may therefore use a reader-facing learning map inspired by common MBSR themes, not an official curriculum. Safe themes include noticing automatic pilot, sensing the body, returning to breath/body/movement, observing stress reactions, distinguishing reaction from response, observing interpersonal reactivity, integrating mindfulness into daily life, and planning continuation.

Do not present these themes as the exact Brown, UMass, or official MBSR sequence unless primary curriculum materials are directly extracted.

## Home practice

### Purpose

Home practice can be framed as a way to bring repeated noticing into daily life, not as a test of discipline or a guarantee of clinical benefit.

### Possible role

It may support familiarity with body scan, sitting meditation, mindful movement, informal mindfulness, and reflection. It can also make practice-related difficulties visible earlier.

### Adherence uncertainty

Parsons et al. 2017 is relevant to home practice, but adherence measures, practice categories, outcomes, participant contexts, and causal inference differ across studies.

### Do not overstate dose-response

Do not claim that more minutes automatically produce better outcomes. Reader-facing content should emphasize safe adjustment, shortening, changing the object of attention, pausing, and seeking support when adverse signs appear.

## Evidence boundaries

- Do not claim MBSR cures stress, anxiety, pain, depression, or illness.
- Do not equate MBSR with generic relaxation.
- Do not equate MBSR with Buddhist retreat practice.
- Do not use MBSR as a replacement for medical care.
- Do not claim a specific week-by-week curriculum is official unless directly verified.
- Do not imply that self-guided reading fully replaces a trained MBSR teacher, group inquiry, intake, or clinical judgment.
