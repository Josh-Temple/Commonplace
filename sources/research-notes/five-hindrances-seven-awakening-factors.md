# Five hindrances and seven awakening factors

## Scope

This note supports cautious reader-facing content about the five hindrances and seven awakening factors in Theravada-related meditation contexts.

It is not a manual for removing hindrances, diagnosing attainments, or reproducing formal Satipaṭṭhāna training.

## Five hindrances

- sensual desire
- ill will
- sloth and torpor
- restlessness and remorse
- doubt

Reader-facing use: a light vocabulary for noticing what is covering, pulling, agitating, dulling, or stalling the mind. Do not use as self-criticism.

## Seven awakening factors

- mindfulness
- investigation of dhammas
- energy
- joy / rapture
- tranquility
- concentration
- equanimity

Reader-facing use: a vocabulary for balance and direction within Buddhist practice, not an attainment checklist or productivity framework.

## Relationship to Satipaṭṭhāna

The existing Satipaṭṭhāna primary-text note records five hindrances and seven awakening factors inside dhammas contemplation. This supports saying that dhammas contemplation includes doctrinal / practice frameworks, not just ordinary thought observation.

MN 118 also connects mindfulness of breathing, the four satipaṭṭhānas, and awakening factors in its broader structure. Reader-facing pages may mention this relationship cautiously without turning it into a self-guided sequence.

## Reader-facing safe framing

- Use the five hindrances as a light map for noticing common obstacles.
- Do not turn them into self-criticism or moral attack.
- Do not prescribe strong practices for removing them.
- Use the seven factors as a vocabulary for balance, not as attainment diagnosis.
- Do not claim awakening factors are psychological hacks.
- If noticing a hindrance intensifies distress, return to external sensory grounding or stop the practice.
